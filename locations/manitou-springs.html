What Makes Mold Different in Historic Manitou Springs Homes

Modern construction uses vapor barriers, pressure-treated wood, and moisture-resistant materials. Manitou Springs' Victorian and Craftsman homes were built with none of these. Here's what that means for mold risk:

Original rubble stone and brick foundations absorb moisture directly. There is no membrane between the soil and the foundation in most pre-1950 Manitou construction. Groundwater and soil vapor migrate continuously into basements and crawl spaces.

Original plaster and lath walls hold moisture differently than drywall. Mold in plaster walls often starts behind the material and works forward — the surface looks fine until the damage is significant.

Old-growth wood in joists and framing is more resistant but not immune. Prolonged moisture exposure in any wood eventually creates mold conditions, and the older structural wood in these homes has had decades of exposure.

Without containment, spores spread. In a small historic home with limited airflow, disturbing mold without proper containment affects the entire living space rapidly.
